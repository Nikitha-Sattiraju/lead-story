Invisible Poisoning

Close to 90,000 children tested positive for unsafe blood lead levels in 2016, according to the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). But that does not give us the full picture.

By Nikitha Sattiraju & Karishma Vanjani

Tens of thousands of children in the country continue to test positive for unsafe blood lead levels. However, CDC data on childhood lead poisoning is an underrepresentation of the extent to which American children are affected. At present, CDC only funds 35 state and local programs to report the number of children that tested positive for high lead levels in their blood. The remaining states may volunteer the information should their state health programs choose to but are not required to do so. As a result, there is no data on childhood lead poisoning for several states as shown in the map below. The latest available data is only until 2016.

According to a study by the the Public Health Institute's California Environmental Health Tracking Program, the number of children with elevated blood lead levels between 1999-2010 was 1.2 million, which is double what the CDC reported. Lead poisoning, even at low levels, can have serious implications for childhood development. It can affect children's IQ, behaviour as well as hearing. However, parents often have a hard time linking these symptoms to lead poisoning and therefore don't get their children tested, according to Jill Samuels, program administrator, the lead poisoning prevention and treatment program at the children's hospital at Montefiore, New York City. As a result, many children are not tested or treated.
